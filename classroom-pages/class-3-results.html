The Outcome and the Better Move

What happened next

The decision created visible and hidden consequences.

The visible outcome

  • Built a hybrid business model: manufacture the high-performing SKUs where the company still held advantage, and import the rest to compete more effectively.
  • Leveraged global partnerships to access advanced technology, improved specifications, and stronger market options.
  • Rationalized brands by removing those that cannibalised each other, creating a more focused market position.
  • Reset the supply chain by building a new team and hiring new skills for global sourcing.
  • Invested in people and built a highly engaged team around the new direction.

The hidden lesson

The strongest move was not only operational, it was psychological. It required leaders to stop treating external pressure as something to control, and start shaping what they could influence. The future is won by knowing what to protect, what to change, and what to let go of before circumstances make the decision for you.

Best option

The strongest move was the one that shaped the future, not just the quarter.

Leadership blind spots

You did not know you had.

Use these after the reveal. Each blind spot helps learners compare their Decision Lab choice against the thinking pattern that may have shaped it.

01 / 06

Confusing motion with control

Staying deeply involved creates the illusion of control, while the few decisions that would actually shift the business remain unmade.

Control question: What decision was only yours to make?

02 / 06

Protecting what once worked

Legacy success can make outdated systems feel safer than they are. The leader starts defending history instead of designing the next model.

Pattern question: What did your first choice try to preserve?

03 / 06

Mistaking complexity for sophistication

Too many brands, plants, priorities, and exceptions can look like scale, but actually hide diluted decisions and slow execution.

Clarity question: What needed to become simpler before it could become stronger?

04 / 06

Letting pressure choose the timeline

When leaders delay the hard call, the market eventually makes the decision for them, usually with fewer options left on the table.

Timing question: What became more expensive the longer leadership waited?

05 / 06

Over-indexing on what cannot be controlled

External forces matter, but when they dominate the conversation, they can quietly become permission to avoid the controllable work.

Focus question: What deserved influence, even if it could not be controlled?

06 / 06

Calling survival a strategy

Stabilizing the present is necessary, but it is not the same as shaping the future. The best move must do both.

Future question: What was the business building toward, not just away from?
